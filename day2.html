Day 2

The First Thing I Misunderstood About You

Today is Monday, so this page also has a little bit of our Monday in it.

The first thing I noticed about you was that you asked too many questions.

Honestly, at that time, it was irritating.

Every discussion had another question. Every integration had another doubt. Every process had another detail you wanted to understand.

I remember thinking: “Who is this girl? Will she even be able to handle Magic Checkout?”

At that time, I only saw the questions.

Later, I understood what those questions actually meant.

They meant you cared. They meant you wanted to understand. They meant you did not want to do things just for the sake of doing them.

You wanted to be good. Not average. Good.

The first thing I misunderstood about you later became one of the things I admired most.

And today, when I see how far you have come professionally, I feel genuinely proud.

Not surprised. Proud.

Because the girl who asked too many questions was never difficult. She was becoming excellent.

Our Monday Kind of Ordinary

Today is Monday, and somehow that makes me think of us.

The office. The fifth floor. Those coffee breaks.

Me with my cigarette and black tea. You with tea, badam tea, chaach, lassi — whatever your mood chose that day.

On paper, these were ordinary moments. But somewhere, these ordinary things became familiar.

Maybe that is how some bonds grow — not through big declarations, but through repeated small moments that start feeling like they belong to only two people.

Maybe we started with work, questions, confusion, and irritation.

But somewhere, slowly, you became someone whose presence felt familiar. Someone whose messages mattered. Someone whose care stayed even after the conversation ended.

That is why this page is still about you, and also a little about us — because some bonds begin in the most ordinary places and still become unforgettable.

That 3–4% We Joke About

I started by saying 1–2%. Then somehow it became 3–4%.

Maybe we kept it as a joke because jokes are safer than honest sentences.

Maybe that tiny percentage became our way of saying: there is something here, but life is complicated, so let us not name it too loudly.

Was I completely imagining that 3–4%, Golu Babu?

I knew some things did not need loud confirmation.

Thank you for saying it in the smallest, safest way.

This does not have to change anything. It can stay here — quiet, safe, and understood.

Haan, maybe I do.

Some things are understood without being said. You have always been very good at that.

I respect what you say. I also understand what life makes difficult to say.

You were never difficult, Kanika Babu. You were sincere. And sincerity looks confusing to people who are not paying enough attention.

Today, you do not have to explain anything. Just read this and smile a little if you can.
